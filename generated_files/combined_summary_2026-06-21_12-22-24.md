**Understanding LLMs, SLMs, and Agent Architectures:**

*   **SLMs vs. LLMs:**
    *   **Architecture:** Small Language Models (SLMs) are typically under 10 billion parameters, running on consumer devices. Large Language Models (LLMs) exceed 10 billion parameters with deeper layers and more attention heads, designed for broad reasoning.
    *   **Task Complexity:** SLMs excel at simple, linear tasks but struggle with complex, multi-step reasoning. LLMs handle difficult math, multi-step code, and long-horizon planning.
    *   **Long Context Recall:** SLMs often lose coherence over extended documents or conversations. LLMs reliably track and connect information across large inputs.
    *   **Latency and Cost:** SLMs offer low response times and significantly lower inference costs on consumer hardware. LLMs require GPUs and incur higher costs per request.
    *   **Deployment and Privacy:** SLMs can be deployed on-device or on-premise, offering enhanced privacy. LLMs are generally cloud-hosted, adding data governance complexity.
    *   **Applications:** SLMs are suitable for on-device assistants, real-time classification, or privacy-sensitive applications. LLMs are best for complex reasoning, agent workflows, or broad knowledge tasks.
*   **Single Agent vs. Multi-Agent Architecture:**
    *   **Single-agent systems** involve one reasoning LLM that plans, executes, and iterates on its own. They are ideal for clear, linear tasks where one agent can manage the entire problem, offering simplicity in building and debugging.
    *   **Multi-agent systems** employ an orchestrator to divide a task into subtasks, routing each to a specialized agent. These are beneficial when subtasks can run in parallel, when independent verification is needed (e.g., one agent writes, another verifies), or for problems too complex for a single agent.
    *   While single agents are cheaper and simpler, they have limitations for complex work. Multi-agent systems offer greater capability and reliability but incur higher coordination costs. A recommended approach is to start with a single agent and transition to a multi-agent system only when context management or reliability becomes a bottleneck.
